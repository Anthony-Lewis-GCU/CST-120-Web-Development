Music

My journey into music started in the third grade, when I joined the school band and picked up the flute for the first time. I didn’t know it then, but that moment would spark a lifelong relationship with sound, rhythm, and creative expression. The flute was my gateway — delicate, precise, and surprisingly challenging for a kid just learning to read sheet music. But I stuck with it, and something about the process of learning, experimenting, and performing began to feel natural to me.

As I grew older, my curiosity expanded beyond the flute. I found myself drawn to other instruments — the piano, guitar, drums, and even the trumpet. I never formally mastered any of them, but that didn’t stop me. I had a knack for figuring things out on my own. Whether it was learning chords on the piano by ear, picking up guitar riffs from songs I loved, or experimenting with drum patterns, I could usually get to an intermediate level without much outside help. I wasn’t chasing perfection — I was chasing connection. Each instrument offered a new way to express myself, and I loved the challenge of unlocking its unique voice.

Eventually, I ventured into music production. I started dabbling with Fruity Loops, mostly creating EDM-style tracks. That opened up a whole new world for me — one where I wasn’t just playing music, I was building it from scratch. Layering synths, tweaking beats, experimenting with effects — it was like painting with sound. I didn’t have a formal background in production, but again, my instincts kicked in. I could feel what worked and what didn’t, and I learned by doing.

Over the years, I’ve listened to just about every genre imaginable. I don’t believe in labeling music as good or bad based solely on style — every genre has its gems and its misses. What matters to me is how the music makes me feel. That said, alternative rock has always held a special place in my heart. There’s something about its raw emotion, its introspective lyrics, and its gritty energy that resonates with me on a deeper level. It hits my soul in a way that few other genres can.

Music has never been just a hobby for me — it’s been a companion, a teacher, and a mirror. I may not be a virtuoso, but I’m a lifelong learner, always exploring, always listening, always creating. My journey is far from over, and I wouldn’t have it any other way.

Music Theory

Flute:

I started playing the flute in 3rd grade, drawn to its silvery tone and expressive range. What began as curiosity quickly turned into commitment—I practiced intensely, mastering fingerings and breath control while exploring school band repertoire. By junior high, I had outpaced the standard curriculum. I tackled more complex pieces, refined my tone, and developed a keen ear for ensemble dynamics.

In 8th grade, all that dedication paid off: I earned a spot in the honors band. It wasn’t just about technical ability—it was a recognition of my musical intuition and discipline. That experience marked a turning point, affirming my place among the top young musicians in the district and deepening my connection to performance.

Piano:

I started piano in freshman year of high school—not as a long-term pursuit, but more as a way to play the songs I loved from movies. I wasn’t interested in scales or theory; I just wanted to recreate those cinematic moments on the keys. So I’d pick a theme, figure it out by ear or sheet music, and play until it felt right.

I didn’t keep studying piano after that year, but for the final project, I wrote my own original song. It was my way of tying everything together—taking what I’d learned and turning it into something personal. That piece was more than just a grade; it was a snapshot of how I connect with music: emotionally, intuitively, and on my own terms.

Guitar:

I’ve played guitar loosely my whole life, mostly because my dad really wanted me to learn it. He was passionate about it, and I gave it a shot out of respect and curiosity. But it never fully clicked for me. I’d pick it up now and then, try a few chords or riffs, but it always felt like I was borrowing someone else’s instrument—not speaking my own musical language.

I never got good with it, and honestly, that’s okay. It just wasn’t for me. I connect more deeply with other instruments, ones that feel intuitive and expressive in a way guitar never did. Still, I appreciate what it taught me—about persistence, about listening, and about finding my own path in music.

Drums:

I bought a drum set in high school and instantly felt most at home behind it. There was something visceral and grounding about the rhythm—the way it connected body and sound. I’d spend hours experimenting, locking into grooves, trying to emulate the energy I heard in my favorite tracks. It felt natural, like the instrument matched my instincts.

But technique was always a challenge. I struggled with consistency, coordination, and the finer points of control. Despite how much I loved the feel of it, I never felt like I got good enough. Eventually, the hobby drifted to the sidelines. Still, drums gave me a deeper appreciation for rhythm and structure, and that influence shows up in everything else I play.

FL Studio:

After high school, I discovered Fruity Loops and everything changed. I started hanging out with DJs, soaking in the energy of live sets and studio sessions. That world pulled me in—hard. I wasn’t just listening anymore; I wanted to build the sound myself. Fruity Loops became my playground for designing EDM tracks, layering synths, tweaking effects, and crafting drops that hit just right.

It wasn’t about formal training—it was instinct, experimentation, and pure passion. I’d lose hours chasing a vibe, sculpting beats, and learning how to make a track move people. That’s where my production journey really began.
